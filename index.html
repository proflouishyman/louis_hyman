Featured book

Temp: How American work became temporary

A history of how deliberate decisions by consultants and executives reshaped job security, long before the apps of the gig economy.

Temp traces how the postwar pillars of stability—unions, corporate stewardship, and regulation—were dismantled in favor of short-term returns. Through stories of consultants, temps, office workers, and laborers, it explains how the American Dream was unmade and why insecurity runs deeper than Uber or downsizing.

“Engaging… A quietly hopeful spin on an economic process that has proved tremendously dislocating for a generation and more of workers.”

— Kirkus Reviews

“A revealing study of the gig economy, which, though it seems new, has long antecedents.”

— Booklist

Why has work become insecure?

Temp explores the history of today's most immediate crisis and shows how a belief in “the market” replaced long-term investment in workers.

About

Louis Hyman

Historian of work and business at the Agora Institute at Johns Hopkins University.

Louis Hyman is the author of Temp, Borrow, Debtor Nation, and other collections and articles. He is currently researching a history of e-commerce.

Originally from Baltimore, he holds a BA in history and mathematics from Columbia University and a PhD in American history from Harvard University. A former Fulbright scholar and McKinsey associate, he is a founding editor of Columbia Studies in the History of U.S. Capitalism and directs the History of Capitalism Summer Camp.

Louis enjoys speaking with book clubs and organizations via Zoom or Skype.
